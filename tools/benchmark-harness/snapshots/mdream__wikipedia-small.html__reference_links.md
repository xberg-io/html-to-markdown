Taxonomic rank between class and family

This article is about the taxonomic rank. For the sequence of species in a taxonomic list, see [taxonomic sequence][119]. For other uses, see [Order][120].

[/wiki/File:Biological_classification_L_Pengo_vflip.svg][122]

*The hierarchy of [biological classification][123]'s eight major [taxonomic ranks][124]. A [class][125] contains one or more orders. Intermediate minor rankings are not shown.*

**Order** ([Latin][126]: *[ordo][127]*) is one of the eight major [hierarchical][128] taxonomic ranks in [Linnaean taxonomy][129]. It is classified between [family][130] and [class][125]. In [biological classification][131], the order is a [taxonomic rank][124] used in the classification of organisms and recognized by the [nomenclature codes][132]. An immediately higher rank, **superorder**, is sometimes added directly above order, with **suborder** directly beneath order. An order can also be defined as a group of related families.

What does and does not belong to each order is determined by a [taxonomist][133], as is whether a particular order should be recognized at all. Often there is no exact agreement, with different taxonomists each taking a different position. There are no hard rules that a taxonomist needs to follow in describing or recognizing an order. Some taxa are accepted almost universally, while others are recognized only rarely.[[1]][134]

The name of an order is usually written with a capital letter.[[2]][135] For some groups of organisms, their orders may follow consistent naming [schemes][136]. Orders of [plants][137], [fungi][138], and [algae][139] use the suffix *-ales* (e.g. [Dictyotales][140]).[[3]][141] Orders of [birds][142] and [fishes][143][[4]][144] use the Latin suffix *-iformes* meaning 'having the form of' (e.g. [Passeriformes][145]), but orders of [mammals][146], [reptiles][147], [amphibians][148] and [invertebrates][149] are not so consistent (e.g. [Artiodactyla][150], [Anura][151], [Crocodylia][152], [Actiniaria][153], [Primates][154]).

### Hierarchy of ranks

[[edit][155]]

#### Zoology

[[edit][156]]

For some [clades][157] covered by the [International Code of Zoological Nomenclature][158], several additional classifications are sometimes used, although not all of these are officially recognized.

| Name       | Latin [prefix][159]                        | Examples                                       |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Magnorder  | *[magnus][160]*, 'large, great, important' | [Boreoeutheria][161]                           |
| Superorder | *[super][162]*, 'above'                    | [Euarchontoglires][163], [Laurasiatheria][164] |
| Grandorder | *[grandis][165]*, 'large'                  | [Euarchonta][166], [Ferungulata][167]          |
| Mirorder   | *[mirus][168]*, 'wonderful, strange'       | [Primatomorpha][169], [Ferae][170]             |
| Order      |                                            | [Primates][171], [Procolophonomorpha][172]     |
| Suborder   | *[sub][173]*, 'under'                      | [Haplorrhini][174], [Procolophonia][175]       |
| Infraorder | *[infra][176]*, 'below'                    | [Simiiformes][177], [Tarsiiformes][178]        |
| Parvorder  | *[parvus][179]*, 'small, unimportant'      | [Catarrhini][180], [Mysticeti][181]            |


In their 1997 classification of [mammals][146], [McKenna][182] and Bell used two extra levels between superorder and order: *grandorder* and *mirorder*.[[5]][183] Michael Novacek (1986) inserted them at the same position. [Michael Benton][184] (2005) inserted them between superorder and magnorder instead.[[6]][185] This position was adopted by *Systema Naturae 2000* and others.

#### Botany

[[edit][186]]

In [botany][187], the ranks of subclass and suborder are secondary ranks pre-defined as respectively above and below the rank of order.[[7]][188] Any number of further ranks can be used as long as they are clearly defined.[[7]][188]

The superorder rank is commonly used, with the ending *-anae* that was initiated by [Armen Takhtajan][189]'s publications from 1966 onwards.[[8]][190]

### History

[[edit][191]]

The order as a distinct rank of biological classification having its own distinctive name (and not just called a *higher genus* (*genus summum*)) was first introduced by the German botanist [Augustus Quirinus Rivinus][192] in his classification of plants that appeared in a series of treatises in the 1690s. [Carl Linnaeus][193] was the first to apply it consistently to the division of all three kingdoms of nature (then [minerals][194], [plants][137], and [animals][195]) in his *[Systema Naturae][196]* (1735, 1st. Ed.).

#### Botany

[[edit][197]]

[/wiki/File:Linnaeus1758-title-page.jpg][199]

*Title page of the 1758 edition of Linnaeus's *Systema Naturæ*.[[9]][200]*

For plants, Linnaeus' orders in the *Systema Naturae* and the *[Species Plantarum][201]* were strictly artificial, introduced to subdivide the artificial classes into more comprehensible smaller groups. When the word *ordo* was first consistently used for natural units of plants, in 19th-century works such as the *[Prodromus Systematis Naturalis Regni Vegetabilis][202]* of [Augustin Pyramus de Candolle][203] and the *[Genera Plantarum][204]* of Bentham & Hooker, it indicated taxa that are now given the rank of family (see [ordo naturalis][205], '*natural order*').

In French botanical publications, from [Michel Adanson][206]'s *Familles naturelles des planets* (1763) and until the end of the 19th century, the word *famille* (plural: *familles*) was used as a French equivalent for this Latin *ordo*. This equivalence was explicitly stated in the [Alphonse Pyramus de Candolle][207]'s *Lois de la nomenclature botanique* (1868), the precursor of the currently used *[International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants][208]*.

In the first international *Rules* of [botanical nomenclature][209] from the [International Botanical Congress][210] of 1905, the word *family* (*familia*) was assigned to the rank indicated by the French *famille*, while order (*ordo*) was reserved for a higher rank, for what in the 19th century had often been named a 'cohort' (*cohors*,[[10]][211] plural *cohortes*).

Some of the plant families still retain the names of Linnaean "natural orders" or even the names of pre-Linnaean natural groups recognized by Linnaeus as orders in his natural classification (e.g. *[Palmae][212]* or *[Labiatae][213]*). Such names are known as [descriptive][214] family names.

#### Zoology

[[edit][215]]

In the field of [zoology][216], the Linnaean orders were used more consistently. That is, the orders in the zoology part of the *Systema Naturae* refer to natural groups. Some of his ordinal names are still in use, e.g. [Lepidoptera][217] (moths and butterflies) and [Diptera][218] (flies, mosquitoes, midges, and gnats).[[11]][219]

#### Virology

[[edit][220]]

In [virology][221], the [International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses][222]'s [virus classification][223] includes fifteen taxonomic ranks to be applied for [viruses][224], [viroids][225] and [satellite nucleic acids][226]: [realm][227], [subrealm][228], [kingdom][229], subkingdom, [phylum][230], [subphylum][231], class, subclass, order, suborder, family, [subfamily][232], genus, [subgenus][233], and species.[[12]][234] There are currently fourteen viral orders, each ending in the suffix *-virales*.[[13]][235]
